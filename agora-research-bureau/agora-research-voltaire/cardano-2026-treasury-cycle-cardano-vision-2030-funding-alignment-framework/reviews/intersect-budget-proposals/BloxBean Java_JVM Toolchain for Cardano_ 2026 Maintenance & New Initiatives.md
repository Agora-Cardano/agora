## AI Rationale

The proposal aligns most directly with **Pillar 2: Adoption & Utility**, specifically the **Developer Experience** section of the Cardano Vision 2030 framework. The framework places open-source maintenance of Cardano SDKs, frameworks, infrastructure, education/migration materials, and compatibility work under Adoption & Utility.

The proposal’s core purpose is to maintain and mature the Java/JVM developer toolchain for Cardano, including Cardano Client Lib, Yaci, Yaci Store, Yaci DevKit, Yano, and JuLC. Its primary target users are Java/Kotlin/Scala developers, enterprise developers, and teams building Cardano applications. Its deliverables focus on SDK maintenance, hard fork readiness, developer tooling, local devnet infrastructure, API compatibility, documentation, tutorials, and smart contract development support. 

Although the proposal contains technical infrastructure work, it is not primarily about L1 protocol improvements, L2 integration, full-node client diversity, cross-chain interoperability, ZK capabilities, or protocol-level security. Therefore, **Infrastructure & Research** is not the strongest formal Vision 2030 classification. Likewise, while the proposal may support ecosystem growth indirectly by helping developers build on Cardano, the substantial deliverables are developer tooling and developer experience rather than a distinct community, talent acquisition, or global engagement program. For that reason, **Secondary Pillar = None**.

## Supporting Evidence

### Evidence 1

The proposal states that BloxBean maintains “a suite of production-grade open-source Java/JVM toolkits for Cardano,” including Cardano Client Lib, Yaci, Yaci Store, and Yaci DevKit, and that these tools are an entry point for Java/Kotlin/Scala engineers to build on Cardano. 

### Evidence 2

The proposal seeks funding for “ongoing maintenance and the maturation of new initiatives,” including regular maintenance, hard fork readiness, stable releases, Yano, and JuLC. This directly matches the Vision 2030 Developer Experience focus on maintaining core SDKs, frameworks, and infrastructure. 

### Evidence 3

The proposal’s roadmap includes CCL 0.8.0, Yaci Store API compatibility, Analytics Store, MCP server, Yaci DevKit updates, Yano devnet-node maturation, and JuLC Java-to-UPLC compiler development. These are developer tooling and compatibility deliverables, not end-user adoption campaigns or governance-process deliverables. 

### Evidence 4

The proposal explicitly says that Yaci DevKit improves Cardano DevEx by simplifying local devnet creation and that Java developers can build on Cardano using familiar Web2 stacks and frameworks. 

### Evidence 5

The Vision 2030 framework’s Pillar 2 includes “Developer Experience,” including open-source incentives for maintaining Cardano SDKs, frameworks, and infrastructure, education/migration materials, and compatibility with common multi-chain tooling.

## Classification Notes

### Primary Classification Justification

**Adoption & Utility** was selected because the proposal’s main strategic function is to reduce friction for developers building on Cardano through maintained SDKs, Java/JVM tooling, compatibility layers, local development infrastructure, documentation, and smart contract development support.

**Infrastructure & Research** was not selected as the Primary Pillar because the proposal does not primarily target protocol scalability, L1 consensus/ledger/networking improvements, L2 settlement architecture, cross-chain state proofs, ZK infrastructure, protocol-critical cryptography, full-node client diversity, or network recovery systems.

**Community & Ecosystem Growth** was not selected as a Secondary Pillar because the community-facing elements, such as documentation, tutorials, examples, and an optional workshop, support the developer tooling work rather than forming a separate substantial workstream focused on talent acquisition, structured ecosystem onboarding, localized engagement, or market narrative.

### Additional Notes

The proposal’s own self-classification includes Pillar 2 and Pillar 4. Under the stricter classification rules used here, Pillar 4 is treated as an indirect or supporting effect rather than a direct and substantial secondary alignment.
